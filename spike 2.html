Official US government publication · nimh.nih.gov
What is OCD?
Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD) is a long-lasting disorder in which a person experiences uncontrollable and recurring thoughts (obsessions), engages in repetitive behaviors (compulsions), or both. People with OCD have time-consuming symptoms that can cause significant distress or interfere with daily life. However, treatment is available to help people manage their symptoms and improve their quality of life.
Signs & symptoms
Obsessions
Repeated thoughts, urges, or mental images that are intrusive, unwanted, and cause anxiety. Common obsessions include:
Fear of germs or contamination
Fear of forgetting, losing, or misplacing something
Fear of losing control over one's behavior
Aggressive thoughts toward others or oneself
Unwanted, forbidden, or taboo thoughts involving sex, religion, or harm
Desire to have things symmetrical or in perfect order
Compulsions
Repetitive behaviors a person feels the urge to do, often in response to an obsession. Common compulsions include:
Excessive cleaning or handwashing
Ordering or arranging things in a particular, precise way
Repeatedly checking things (e.g., that the door is locked or the oven is off)
Compulsive counting
Praying or repeating words silently
Not everyone who has obsessive thoughts or performs repetitive behaviors has OCD. For a diagnosis, these symptoms must be time-consuming, distressing, or interfering with daily life.
Risk factors
OCD is a common disorder affecting adults, adolescents, and children worldwide. Risk factors include:
Genetics. People with a first-degree relative with OCD have a higher chance of developing OCD themselves. The risk is higher if the relative developed OCD as a child or teenager.
Brain structure and functioning. Research has shown differences in the frontal cortex and subcortical structures of the brain in people with OCD.
Environment. Childhood trauma or other stressful events have been associated with an increased risk of developing OCD.
Diagnosis
A doctor or mental health professional can diagnose OCD. They will ask about your symptoms and how long you have had them. A diagnosis of OCD requires the presence of obsessions, compulsions, or both that are time-consuming (taking more than one hour a day), cause significant distress, or interfere with your daily life.
Treatment
OCD is typically treated with medication, psychotherapy, or a combination of both. Most patients with OCD benefit from treatment.
Psychotherapy
Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT) is the most effective type of psychotherapy for OCD. A specific type of CBT called Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP) is particularly helpful. In ERP, people spend time in situations that trigger their obsessions but are helped to refrain from their usual compulsive response. This treatment has been associated with changes in brain activity.
Medication
Doctors often prescribe serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SRIs) and selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) to help treat OCD.
Other treatments
For people with OCD who do not respond to standard treatments, newer approaches are available including deep brain stimulation (DBS) and transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS). Talk to a healthcare provider to understand all available options.
Find help
If you think you or someone you know may have OCD, talk to a doctor or mental health professional. To find a specialist:
IOCDF directory: iocdf.org/find-help
Online ERP therapy: treatmyocd.com
Crisis line: Call or text 988

Questions to ask before booking
Not every therapist who says they treat OCD is ERP-trained. Ask these before committing:
"Do you use Exposure and Response Prevention as your primary approach?" — If they say yes to CBT but aren't sure about ERP, look elsewhere.
"How many OCD clients do you currently see?" — You want someone for whom OCD is a speciality, not a side interest.
"Are you familiar with Pure O and intrusive thought subtypes?" — Tests whether they know OCD beyond contamination and checking.
"Will we do in-session exposures, or is it mostly talking?" — Real ERP involves doing exposures in session, not just talking about them.
"Do you provide reassurance during sessions?" — The correct answer is no. Any therapist who reassures is not doing ERP correctly.
Red flags to avoid
Leave if your therapist:
✗ Analyses the content of your intrusive thoughts
✗ Reassures you that your fears won't come true
✗ Focuses only on why you have the thoughts
✗ Never does actual exposures with you
✗ Suggests the thoughts reveal something about you
✗ Has you journal about or process intrusive thoughts repeatedly

Cognitive distortion
Thought-action fusion
Thought-action fusion (TAF) is the belief that having a thought is the same as doing it — or that thinking something can make it happen. It is the engine that powers the "if I think it, something bad will happen" spiral.
Two types — both are distortions
Type 1 — Likelihood TAF
"If I think about something bad happening, I make it more likely to happen."

Example: "If I imagine my family in an accident, I'm causing it to become real." This is the direct link between thought and consequence that does not exist in reality.
Type 2 — Moral TAF
"Thinking about doing something bad is just as wrong as actually doing it."

Example: "I had a thought about harming someone — that makes me as guilty as someone who actually did it." This is why an intrusive thought can feel like a moral catastrophe.
The truth about TAF
Thoughts are mental events. They have no causal power over the physical world. Thinking cannot make things happen.
A thought about harm is not a desire for harm. The horror the thought causes is the evidence — people who genuinely want harm feel satisfaction, not dread.
Billions of people have intrusive thoughts identical to OCD obsessions and never act on them. The thought itself is neurological noise — not a moral verdict.
TAF is what gives OCD its power. It turns a meaningless thought into an emergency requiring a response. Remove the story, remove the emergency.
The compulsion feels like it "prevents" the bad outcome — but that just confirms the TAF belief and makes it stronger next time.
The TAF test
Ask yourself: if you stood here and thought about a pink elephant for 30 seconds, would one appear? Of course not. Thoughts and reality are separate. The same principle applies to every thought OCD targets.
"A thought is a thought. It is not a lever. It cannot move the world."
ERP demonstrates through experience that the feared outcome doesn't happen when you don't respond to the thought. That is how the TAF belief weakens — not through argument, but through experience.
Cognitive distortion
Magical thinking OCD
Magical thinking OCD is the belief that your thoughts, words, or actions can influence events in the world in ways that defy normal cause and effect. It is built on thought-action fusion — and it turns everyday thoughts into potential catastrophes.
What it sounds like
"If I think about something bad happening to my family, I've made it more likely."
"If I don't do this ritual correctly, something terrible will happen and it will be my fault."
"I had a bad thought — I need to neutralize it before something goes wrong."
"If I said that word, or thought that thing, I've set something in motion."
Why the ritual makes it worse
The ritual appears to work. You think the bad thought, you do the ritual, nothing bad happens — so your brain records: "The ritual prevented it." But nothing bad would have happened anyway. The ritual didn't prevent anything. It just confirmed the false belief and made it stronger.
The compulsion trap
Every time you complete a ritual to "undo" a thought, you teach your brain that the thought was dangerous and the ritual was necessary. The belief grows stronger, not weaker. The only way out is to let the thought be there without the ritual.
The clinical truth
Your thoughts have no causal connection to events in the world. None. This is not a belief — it is physics.
The thought arriving is automatic and involuntary. You did not choose it and you are not responsible for it.
The anxiety the thought produces is real — but anxiety is not a signal that the thought is dangerous. It is OCD's alarm. The alarm is broken.
People with OCD are statistically among the least likely to act on intrusive thoughts. The horror is protective, not predictive.
"My thoughts cannot move the world. They cannot harm anyone. They are noise — not instructions."
Treatment: ERP for magical thinking means allowing the thought to exist without the ritual, and sitting with the uncertainty — not because you performed a ritual, but because thoughts don't cause harm.
Cognitive distortion
Inflated responsibility
Inflated responsibility is the deeply held belief that you have unique power to cause or prevent harm — and a unique moral duty to do so. It is what transforms thought-action fusion and magical thinking from an idea into an emergency.
Two parts working together
Part 1 — Overestimating your power to cause harm
The belief that your thoughts, words, or minor actions can directly lead to terrible consequences for others — even when those outcomes are completely outside your control.
Part 2 — Overestimating your duty to prevent harm
The belief that you are uniquely responsible for preventing bad things from happening — that if something goes wrong and you didn't do everything possible to stop it, it is your fault.
What it sounds like
"If something bad happens to my family and I had a bad thought beforehand, it's my fault."

OCD themes
Common forms OCD takes
OCD attaches to whatever matters most to you.
Harm OCD
Intrusive thoughts about harming yourself or others — despite no desire to do so. The horror is what makes them OCD, not intent.
Religious / scrupulosity OCD
Obsessive fear of sinning or being morally impure. Typically affects deeply devout people — OCD targets faith because it matters most.
Contamination OCD
Fear of germs, illness, or contaminating others. Compulsions include excessive washing and avoidance.
Magical thinking OCD
The belief that thoughts, words, or actions can cause or prevent real-world harm — driven by thought-action fusion and inflated responsibility. Compulsions serve to "undo" the thought, which paradoxically makes the belief stronger.
Pure O — purely obsessional
OCD with primarily mental compulsions — reviewing, reassuring, neutralizing. Coined by Dr. Steven Phillipson. Often the most isolating form.
Relationship OCD (ROCD)
Obsessive doubt about a relationship. The doubt is OCD-generated, not reality.
Identity / sexual orientation OCD
Obsessive questioning of one's identity. The distress and uncertainty are OCD-driven — not a hidden truth.
Existential OCD
Relentless questioning about reality, meaning, consciousness. The loop itself is the OCD mechanism.
False memory OCD
Obsessive fear that you did or said something terrible. OCD manufactures uncertainty where none needs to exist.

The OCD loop
How OCD feeds itself
Once you see the mechanism, you can start to step outside it.
1
The spike — An intrusive thought arrives. Your brain fires an alarm: "This is dangerous. Do something."
2
The anxiety — Genuine physical distress — racing heart, dread, guilt. The feelings are real. The threat is not.
3
The compulsion — You check, review, reassure. It works briefly. Relief floods in.
4
The confirmation — By responding, you told your brain the spike was real. It sends it again — louder next time.
5
The way out — Not responding. Choosing irrelevance. Letting anxiety peak without acting. This is ERP.
"Every compulsion resets the loop. Every non-response weakens it. That is the whole game."

The science
Why your body makes OCD louder or quieter
OCD is a brain condition — but the brain runs on a body. Five physiological systems directly and measurably affect OCD symptom severity. Understanding them changes how you approach recovery.
Sleep — the cortisol cycle
Cortisol should peak in the morning and drop by evening. Poor sleep disrupts this rhythm, keeping cortisol elevated at night. Elevated evening cortisol directly amplifies amygdala reactivity — which means OCD spikes are more intense and more frequent. Sleep is also when fear extinction from ERP consolidates. One bad night measurably changes your threat threshold the next day.
Exercise — BDNF and cortisol reset
A 2023 meta-analysis found aerobic exercise produces a large reduction in OCD symptoms (effect size 1.33) — larger than many medications. Exercise raises BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor) which supports the neuroplasticity ERP depends on. It also normalises cortisol and activates the vagus nerve. Even 20 minutes of moderate aerobic exercise produces measurable same-day effects.
Gut — serotonin and inflammation
Approximately 90% of the body's serotonin is made in the gut. SSRIs — the primary medication for OCD — work on the serotonin system. OCD patients show lower gut microbiome diversity and elevated inflammatory markers that correlate with symptom severity. Fermented foods, fibre, and reduced ultra-processed food intake directly improve the gut-brain signalling that affects OCD.
HRV — vagal tone and the amygdala
Heart rate variability (HRV) reflects how well your prefrontal cortex is regulating your amygdala — the exact circuit that OCD disrupts. Low HRV means the brake on the alarm system is weak. HRV is raised by: slow exhale breathing, cold water exposure, exercise, meditation, and consistent sleep. Every tool in this app that slows your exhale is directly raising your HRV.
Hormones — the 30% factor
Hormonal fluctuations trigger or worsen OCD symptoms in approximately 30% of women — particularly during the premenstrual phase, pregnancy, and postpartum periods. Cortisol, estrogen, and testosterone all directly interact with OCD-related circuits. Tracking symptom patterns against your cycle can reveal enormous amounts about why some days are harder than others.
None of this replaces ERP. But all of it changes the conditions in which ERP happens — making it more or less effective. Build the physical foundations and ERP works better.

Crisis support
Sustained high distress. Let's work through this step by step.
You are not in danger.
OCD creates the feeling of emergency without the reality of it. The distress is real. The threat is not.
1
Name it out loud
Say or think: "This is OCD. My brain is in a false alarm state. I am physically safe right now."
2
Ground your body
Press both feet flat on the floor. Name 5 things you can see. Take one slow breath — 4 in, hold 2, 6 out.
3
Do not ritualise
Every compulsion at this level feeds the next spike. If the urge is unbearable, delay it by 5 minutes and restart this list.
4
Change your environment
Stand up. Move to a different room. Splash cold water on your face. The nervous system responds to physical change.
5
Start a live ERP session
Use the structure to channel the energy into recovery rather than rituals.

Family &
partner mode
For the people who love someone with OCD.
When they are in a spike right now
✓ Say this
"I can see you're really struggling right now. I'm here."
"I'm not going to reassure you because I know it doesn't actually help — but I'm right beside you."
"You don't have to do anything right now. Just breathe."
"This will pass. You've got through this before."
✗ Don't say this
"You'll be fine" — this is reassurance. It feeds the loop.
"Just don't think about it" — impossible for OCD.
"That doesn't make sense" — invalidates without helping.
Helping them avoid triggers — this is accommodation.
Why reassurance makes it worse
When you reassure someone with OCD, the anxiety drops briefly — then returns stronger. You've confirmed that the threat was real enough to need reassurance. The compulsion (seeking reassurance) was reinforced. Over time, they need more reassurance to get the same relief.
The loving response is to refuse to reassure while staying present and warm.
Accommodation — the invisible trap
Accommodation means adjusting family routines, answering repeated questions, helping avoid triggers, or participating in rituals to reduce their distress. It feels kind. It maintains OCD.
Work with an ERP-trained therapist to gradually reduce accommodation. Stopping suddenly without guidance can cause a crisis.
Your own wellbeing matters too
Living with someone who has OCD is hard. Compassion fatigue is real. You are not responsible for managing their OCD — you are responsible for supporting them in managing it themselves.
